Project Journey

I worked on developing an Adaptive Cruise Control ACC system with the goal of maintaining a one second time headway from the vehicle in front, and the journey turned out to be far more insightful than I initially expected. I began with a simple idea of minimizing relative velocity using a PID controller, assuming that matching the speed of the lead vehicle would naturally maintain a safe gap. While this worked for basic scenarios, it quickly failed when tested under varied conditions, making it clear that velocity alone was not enough. I then shifted my approach to a time headway based control strategy, where I calculated the desired distance using the ego vehicle speed and generated a spacing error that was fed into a controller.

The initial implementation caused jerky behavior, which led me to design a cascaded PID architecture, where one controller corrected spacing error and generated a velocity reference, and another controller ensured a smooth velocity response. This significantly improved stability and comfort, but real world scenarios such as lane cut in, cut out, and the absence of a lead vehicle required a more adaptive solution. I developed a hybrid control system that dynamically switched between time gap control and constant velocity control based on filtered radar inputs received through CAN communication.

Despite significant setbacks, including incompatible CAN drivers, I rebuilt the entire ACC system from scratch using Python and ROS. I was deeply involved in hardware and software integration, debugging real time issues, and solving a critical throttle problem by identifying a minimum priming signal of 0.8 volts through CAN. This effort contributed to securing an overall Rank 2 in the competition, teaching me how control theory, embedded systems, and real-world constraints come together under pressure.
